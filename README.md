# AI Maturity Through a Behavioral Lens

## Why Most AI Adoption Programs Fail — and What to Do Instead

**Date:** 2026-05-09

---

## The Core Insight

Most organizations approach AI adoption as a technology rollout or a training problem. They buy tools, run workshops, send emails encouraging people to "embrace AI," and wonder why adoption stalls.

The reason is simple: **AI adoption is a behavior change problem, not a technology problem.** And behavior change has decades of research behind it — research that most AI programs completely ignore.

The single most important finding: **the environment is the bottleneck at every level.** People are not resisting AI. They are responding rationally to environments that make AI use ambiguous, invisible, unrewarded, or unsafe. Fix the environment and the behavior follows.

---

## The Four Levels of AI Maturity

The following levels come from [The AI Transformation Model](https://notion-ai-assessment.vercel.app/), developed by John Hurley at Notion — one of the most widely adopted mental models for how organizations progress in their AI use (used by 2,000+ teams).

| Level | Name | What It Looks Like |
|-------|------|-------------------|
| 1 | AI as a Thought Partner | Help people explore ideas and improve decisions |
| 2 | AI as an Assistant | Complete individual tasks faster, save employee time |
| 3 | AI as Teammates | Automate repetitive work, increase team efficiency |
| 4 | AI as the System | Run critical workflows, scale organizational capacity |

Each level represents a qualitative shift in how AI is used, who's involved, and what barriers dominate. The barriers evolve from social permission (Level 1) to structural defaults (Level 2) to infrastructure and role ambiguity (Level 3) to political dynamics and organizational identity (Level 4).

The model resonates because it matches how adoption actually unfolds in practice. But knowing *where you are* is only half the challenge. The harder question is: **why do organizations get stuck at each level, and what actually moves them forward?**

That's what this analysis answers.

## The Diagnostic Tool: Change Lenses and Actions

To analyze why organizations get stuck at each level, we used [Change Lenses and Actions](https://github.com/johnpcutler/change-lenses-and-actions) — an open-source, seven-step behavior diagnosis framework built on COM-B (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation → Behavior), the Behaviour Change Wheel, and the Behaviour Change Technique Taxonomy v1. The same frameworks used in public health, clinical behavior change, and organizational psychology.

The framework works by applying structured diagnostic lenses to each behavioral challenge:

| Component | What It Provides |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Behavior Canvas** | Precisely defines the target behavior: who, will do what, to what extent, in what context, for what outcome |
| **Four diagnostic lens files** | Spanning 6 COM-B branches: Psychological Capability, Physical Capability, Physical Opportunity, Social Opportunity, Reflective Motivation, Automatic Motivation |
| **30+ sub-lenses** | Each with its own dimensional scales — from "knowledge and skill acquisition" to "norms and normative climate" to "habit and automaticity" |
| **150+ dimensional scales** | Specific continua (e.g., "declarative knowledge: thin factual understanding → rich conceptual understanding") that make diagnosis precise |
| **Practitioner worksheet** | Synthesizes findings, ranks COM-B branches, identifies cross-lens tensions and leverage points |
| **Intervention mapping** | Maps diagnosed barriers to 9 Behaviour Change Wheel intervention functions and 93 specific Behaviour Change Techniques |

The seven-step pipeline: (1) define the behavior precisely, (2) research through four lenses (capability, physical opportunity, social opportunity, motivation), (3) synthesize in a practitioner worksheet, (4) map to interventions, (5) deliver a plain-language summary, (6) feedback loop for refinement, (7) detailed report or action plan on request.

For this analysis, we ran the full diagnostic independently on each of the four AI maturity levels — examining both the adoption behavior (why organizations struggle to reach that level) and the progression blocker (why they get stuck and can't advance to the next). The combined report synthesizes findings across all four.

## What These Documents Contain

This package includes two companion documents:

### 1. The Behavioral Diagnostic ([combined-com-b-report.md](combined-com-b-report.md))

**What it is:** A rigorous analysis of why organizations struggle to adopt each AI maturity level and why they get stuck and can't progress to the next.

**What it answers:**
- What are the real barriers at each level? (Not what people assume — what behavioral science reveals.)
- How do barriers change as organizations advance?
- Why does what worked at one level become an obstacle at the next?
- What recurring patterns appear regardless of maturity level?
- Which intervention strategies have the highest leverage at each stage?

**Key findings:**

1. **Opportunity dominates at every level.** The ranking is always Opportunity > Capability > Motivation. The environment matters more than skills or attitudes.

2. **The intervention function shifts as you climb.** Modelling (showing examples) works at Level 1. Enablement (making things easier) works at Levels 2–3. Environmental Restructuring (changing the system itself) is required at Level 4.

3. **Persuasion ranks last everywhere.** You cannot argue, incentivize, or campaign your way to AI maturity. Structural and social interventions always outperform motivational messaging.

4. **Coercion is universally counterproductive.** Mandating AI adoption creates compliance theater at Level 1 and active political resistance at Level 4. It fails at every level, for different reasons.

5. **Each level transition is a qualitative shift.** You cannot "do more of what worked" to advance. The skills, governance, and organizational structures that succeed at Level N actively block progress to Level N+1.

### 2. The Action Plan ([ai-maturity-action-plan.md](ai-maturity-action-plan.md))

**What it is:** A concrete, phased plan to move an organization through all four levels over approximately 6–8 quarters (18–24 months).

**What it answers:**
- What specific actions should we take at each phase?
- Who owns what?
- How do we know when we're ready to advance?
- What will go wrong and how do we respond?
- How do the phases overlap and sequence?

**Key features:**

1. **Phased by quarter** with overlapping timelines — advanced teams start the next phase while others are still in the current one.

2. **Each phase follows the same sequence:** create the conditions (fix the environment) → build the capability (through practice, not training) → sustain the motivation (through identity-compatible framing and visible success).

3. **Clear readiness criteria** — explicit checklists for when to advance rather than arbitrary timelines.

4. **Watchpoints at every phase** — predictable failure modes and how to detect and respond to them early.

5. **Anti-patterns to avoid** — the most common mistakes organizations make, drawn directly from the behavioral diagnostic.

## The Three Rules

If you remember nothing else:

1. **Fix the environment before the people.** Training and persuasion are waste when the environment is neutral or hostile. Make the behavior easy, visible, and rewarded first.

2. **Never skip levels.** Each level builds foundational capability and organizational muscle that the next level depends on. Jumping from individual tool use to enterprise AI-native operations produces expensive failures.

3. **Match the intervention to the level.** Lunch-and-learns don't solve agent-building capability gaps. Workflow tools don't solve political dynamics. The intervention must match the nature of the barrier at the specific level you're working on.

---

## What This Is Not

- This is **not a technology roadmap.** It does not prescribe specific AI tools, platforms, or vendors. Technology choices matter, but they're not the bottleneck.
- This is **not a training curriculum.** While capability-building appears at every phase, the approach is scaffolded practice on real work, not classroom education.
- This is **not a one-size-fits-all timeline.** The quarter estimates are directional. Organizational size, starting point, industry context, and executive commitment all affect pace.
- This is **not a mandate.** The single most consistent finding is that mandating AI adoption is counterproductive. This plan enables and supports — it does not coerce.
