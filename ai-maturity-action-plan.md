# AI Maturity Action Plan: From Thought Partner to System

> **TL;DR:** A phased plan to advance an organization through four AI maturity levels over 6–8 quarters. Each phase creates the conditions, builds the capability, and sustains the motivation for the next. The key principle: fix the environment first, demonstrate before educating, and never skip levels.

**Maturity model:** [The AI Transformation Model](https://notion-ai-assessment.vercel.app/) by John Hurley, Notion  
**Diagnostic tool:** [Change Lenses and Actions](https://github.com/johnpcutler/change-lenses-and-actions) — seven-step behavior diagnosis framework  
**Based on:** COM-B behavioral diagnostics cross-level synthesis (see [combined-com-b-report.md](combined-com-b-report.md))  
**Timeline:** ~6–8 quarters (18–24 months), pace depends on organizational size and starting point  
**Date:** 2026-05-09

---

## How to Read This Plan

Each phase follows the same structure:

- **Focus:** What forces this phase addresses and why
- **Concrete changes:** Specific actions — tools, process changes, environment changes, social structures
- **Who owns it:** Roles or teams responsible
- **Success signals:** What improves if this phase works; how to know it's time to advance
- **Watchpoints:** What could go wrong and what would indicate the plan needs adjustment

**The phases are not purely sequential.** Later phases begin before earlier ones fully complete. But each phase's *foundation work* must be solid before the next phase's *scaling work* can begin.

## Phase 1: Permission and First Wins (Quarter 1–2)

### Level: AI as a Thought Partner — individual, ad-hoc AI exploration

> **TL;DR:** Create social permission to experiment. Make leaders visibly use AI. Help 30–50% of knowledge workers get one genuine first win with AI on real work. Publish lightweight guidelines that enable rather than restrict.

### Focus

The primary blockers at this level are social: nobody has explicitly said "it's okay to use AI," success is invisible, and senior professionals feel identity threat. The environment is passively ambiguous — people aren't opposed, they just don't have permission. Fix the permission vacuum before investing in training.

### Concrete Changes

**Month 1–2: Create the conditions (Environmental Restructuring + Modelling)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Leader modeling sessions | 3–5 senior leaders publicly share their own AI use in team meetings — including imperfect results, false starts, and what they learned. Not polished demos; authentic "here's what I tried this week." |
| Publish permissive guidelines | A one-page document that says what's allowed, what's not (data sensitivity boundaries), and explicitly names that AI use is encouraged. Clarity removes ambiguity — most people won't start until they know the boundaries. |
| Make success visible | Create a low-friction sharing channel (Slack channel, weekly 2-minute standup slot) where anyone can share "I used AI for X and here's what happened." Not mandatory — just visible. |
| Remove tool friction | Ensure every knowledge worker has access to at least one capable AI tool with a clear, frictionless login. If people need IT tickets or manager approval, they won't start. |

**Month 3–4: Build capability (Enablement + Education)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Scaffolded first-win experiences | Pair people with a specific, low-stakes task from their actual work (not a toy exercise) and walk them through using AI for it. Target: each person succeeds once on something real within their first session. Use existing early adopters as peer coaches — not external trainers. |
| Mental model primers | Short (15-minute) sessions that teach three things: how to frame a good prompt, how to iterate, and how to recognize when AI output is shallow or wrong. Not comprehensive "AI literacy" — just enough to get started safely. |
| Peer learning circles | Groups of 4–6 people who meet biweekly to share what they've tried. Structured around "show and tell" not instruction. Creates social reinforcement and surfaces practical patterns. |

**Month 5–6: Sustain motivation (Persuasion + continued Modelling)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Identity reframing for holdouts | For senior professionals who resist ("AI isn't for someone at my level"), one-on-one conversations that reframe AI as a thinking amplifier, not a replacement for expertise. Peer examples from other senior leaders who've adopted. |
| Celebrate diversity of use cases | Publicly highlight non-obvious use cases — strategy brainstorming, meeting prep, writing critique, data interpretation. Counter the narrative that AI is "just for coding or simple tasks." |
| Retrospective: what changed? | At end of phase, surface concrete stories: "Before AI, this took me 3 hours. Now it takes 45 minutes." Make the invisible visible. |

### Who Owns It

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| Executive sponsor | Models AI use publicly; sets the tone that this is welcome |
| Internal comms / change lead | Coordinates messaging, sharing channels, and storytelling |
| Early adopters (peer coaches) | Deliver scaffolded first-win sessions; facilitate peer learning circles |
| IT / tool administration | Removes access friction; ensures tools are provisioned and working |

### Success Signals — Ready to Advance When:

- [ ] 30–50% of knowledge workers use AI at least weekly for real work
- [ ] Multiple teams have visible, shared examples of AI use
- [ ] People can articulate when AI is helpful and when it's not (calibration)
- [ ] No one is confused about whether they're "allowed" to use AI
- [ ] At least 3 leaders regularly model AI use in visible settings
- [ ] Organic sharing has emerged — people voluntarily show each other what they've found

### Watchpoints

| Risk | Signal | Response |
|------|--------|----------|
| Compliance theater | People say they use AI but actually don't; usage metrics are low | Focus on removing friction, not adding pressure. Ask what's blocking real use. |
| Identity backlash from seniors | Senior professionals vocally dismiss AI; this suppresses adoption in their teams | Prioritize one-on-one peer conversations with respected adopters, not public mandates |
| Tool access becomes the excuse | "I can't get access" persists beyond month 1 | Escalate tool provisioning as a blocking dependency |
| Guidelines are too restrictive | Legal/compliance creates rules that effectively prohibit useful AI interactions | Renegotiate: the guidelines should enable, not restrict. Use "what's the minimum restriction needed?" |

## Phase 2: Embed and Normalize (Quarter 2–4)

### Level: AI as an Assistant — embedded in daily tools, saving individual time

> **TL;DR:** Shift AI from ad-hoc personal exploration to embedded team-level practice. Assign ownership. Make AI the default for 2–3 workflows per team. Create visibility into gains. Build if-then habits that stick.

### Focus

The transition from Level 1 to Level 2 requires moving from "some people sometimes use AI" to "AI is how we do certain things now." The blockers shift from social permission to structural defaults — the environment needs to actively support AI use, not just passively allow it. Individual productivity gains are invisible without intentional measurement and sharing. Incentive structures and role ownership must catch up.

### Concrete Changes

**Month 1–3: Create the conditions (Environmental Restructuring)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Assign AI Integration Leads | Every team designates one person who owns identifying 2–3 team workflows where AI becomes the default approach. Not a full-time role — 10–20% time allocation with explicit mandate and cover from their manager. |
| Workflow audit | Each team identifies their top 5 time-consuming repetitive knowledge tasks. For each, answer: "Could AI do 60%+ of this? What would it take?" Prioritize by volume × ease of transition. |
| Restructure defaults | For selected workflows, change the default process to include AI. Example: meeting notes are AI-generated and human-edited (not human-generated from scratch). First draft of weekly reports uses AI. Customer research starts with AI synthesis. |
| Visibility dashboards | Create a simple, team-visible tracker of AI-assisted work: what tasks are being done with AI, estimated time saved, quality maintained. Not for surveillance — for social proof and organizational learning. |
| Incentive alignment check | Audit: are managers recognizing AI-assisted efficiency? Or are people penalized for "not doing it themselves"? Surface and fix any contradictions between "use AI" messaging and actual reward structures. |

**Month 4–6: Build capability (Training + Enablement)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| If-then habit formation | For each embedded workflow, create explicit triggers: "When you sit down to write a weekly update, open AI first and give it your bullet points." Anchor AI use to existing behavioral cues. Practice until it's automatic, not deliberate. |
| Graded skill-building on real work | Move beyond "first wins" to building depth. AI Integration Leads run monthly skill sessions using actual team work: longer prompts, chain-of-thought, multi-step tasks, evaluation of AI output quality. |
| Calibration training | Teach people to calibrate trust: when to accept AI output, when to verify, when to reject. Build the judgment layer that separates productive use from dangerous over-reliance. |
| Cross-team pattern sharing | Monthly cross-team showcase: each AI Integration Lead shares their team's best new AI workflow. Creates lateral learning and prevents teams from solving the same problems independently. |

**Month 7–9: Sustain and formalize (Modelling + Governance)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Shared standards (lightweight) | Based on accumulated experience (not theory), document: "Here's how we use AI for [task type]. Here's what good looks like. Here are the pitfalls." Living documents, not rigid SOPs. |
| Include AI fluency in development conversations | Not "do you use AI enough?" but "how has AI changed how you work? What have you learned? Where do you want to go deeper?" Normalize it as a professional growth dimension. |
| Address identity and craft concerns | For roles with strong craft identity (writers, designers, analysts): facilitate conversations about how AI changes the craft rather than replacing it. "AI handles the first draft so you can focus on insight and judgment." |

### Who Owns It

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| AI Integration Leads (per team) | Identify workflows, drive adoption, facilitate skill sessions, report progress |
| Team managers | Protect time allocation for Integration Leads; align incentives; model AI use themselves |
| Operations / workflow design | Support workflow audits; help redesign processes to be AI-default |
| L&D / enablement team | Provide training materials, run calibration workshops, curate cross-team showcases |

### Success Signals — Ready to Advance When:

- [ ] Every team has 2–3 workflows where AI is the default (not optional extra)
- [ ] Time savings are measurable and visible at team level
- [ ] AI Integration Leads have clear ownership and protected time
- [ ] Shared standards exist for common task types (living documents, not rigid)
- [ ] People can distinguish good from bad AI output and calibrate trust appropriately
- [ ] New hires are onboarded into AI-default workflows as "how we do things here"
- [ ] Cross-team sharing happens organically, not just in scheduled showcases

### Watchpoints

| Risk | Signal | Response |
|------|--------|----------|
| "Everyone uses Copilot but nothing changed" | Individual adoption is high but team processes haven't shifted | Refocus on workflow redesign, not individual tool use. The unit of change is the team process, not the person. |
| AI Integration Leads have no real authority | They can suggest but not change anything; the role is ceremonial | Give explicit mandate + manager air cover. If they can't actually change workflows, the role is theater. |
| Over-reliance without calibration | People accept all AI output uncritically; quality drops | Prioritize calibration training. Create visible feedback loops where AI errors are caught and shared (not punished). |
| Standards imposed too early | Governance written by people without deep experience creates rules that block effective use | Standards should follow practice, not precede it. Only document what's been proven through experience. |

## Phase 3: Scaffold, Protect, and Govern (Quarter 4–6)

### Level: AI as Teammates — autonomous multi-step agents that automate repetitive work

> **TL;DR:** Shift from AI assisting individual tasks to AI autonomously executing multi-step workflows. Build agent-design capability in non-engineering teams. Resolve role ambiguity (who builds, owns, monitors agents?). Create safe-to-fail experimentation zones with evolving governance.

### Focus

This is the hardest transition for most organizations. The capability jump from "using AI tools" to "building and monitoring AI agents" is qualitative, not quantitative. Prompting skills don't transfer. Non-engineering teams are structurally excluded unless deliberately supported. Role ambiguity ("is this IT's job or mine?") freezes action. Governance is either premature (blocks experimentation) or absent (allows unsafe agents). The environment must be deliberately restructured to make agent-building possible for teams beyond engineering.

### Concrete Changes

**Month 1–3: Create the conditions (Environmental Restructuring + Enablement)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Resolve role ownership | Explicitly answer: who can build agents? Who owns them in production? Who monitors for failures? Publish a clear RACI. If non-engineering teams are expected to build agents, they need platforms, support, and permission — not just encouragement. |
| Build or buy agent platforms | Provide low-code/no-code agent-building tools that non-technical teams can use. If the only path to agents requires Python, you've structurally excluded 80% of the organization. |
| Create safe-to-fail experimentation zones | Designate specific workflows where teams can experiment with agents without risking production systems. Clear boundaries: what's okay to automate experimentally, what requires more oversight. |
| Establish lightweight initial governance | Minimum viable governance for agents: what data can agents access? What actions can they take autonomously vs. requiring human approval? What happens when an agent fails? Keep it simple — it will evolve. |
| Protect experimentation time | Explicitly allocate time for agent-building exploration. If it competes with delivery deadlines, delivery always wins and agents never get built. |

**Month 4–6: Build capability (Training + Modelling)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Scaffolded first-agent experiences | Each team builds their first production agent with dedicated support. Start with the simplest possible workflow: a clear trigger, well-defined steps, low-stakes output. Target: first working agent in under 2 weeks. |
| Agent-design training for non-engineers | Teach the fundamentals that are different from prompting: decomposing workflows into steps, defining triggers and conditions, specifying success criteria, designing failure modes and human escalation paths. |
| Cross-team demonstrations | Teams that succeed early demonstrate their agents to other teams. Crucially: demonstrations from non-technical teams, not just engineering. This breaks the "agents are for developers" mental model. |
| Failure sharing and learning | Create a psychologically safe space to share agent failures. "Our agent did X wrong because we didn't account for Y. Here's what we learned." Normalize iteration and imperfection. |

**Month 7–9: Scale and govern (Environmental Restructuring + Education)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Evolve governance based on experience | After 6 months of experimentation, governance gaps and needs will be clear. Update rules based on what actually went wrong, not hypothetical risks. |
| Agent monitoring and observability | Build or adopt tooling that makes agent behavior visible: what did it do, what decisions did it make, where did it need human intervention? Essential for trust-building at scale. |
| Expand agent ownership broadly | Move from "pilot teams" to "any team can build agents for their workflows." Support with shared platforms, templates, and documentation from early adopters. |
| Address role evolution | Some roles will change significantly as agents handle their repetitive work. Proactively support the conversation: "What does your role become when the agent handles the routine?" Not as a threat — as an opportunity to focus on higher-value work. |

### Who Owns It

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| Platform / AI engineering team | Build and maintain agent-building platforms; provide technical support to non-engineering teams |
| Team leads / AI Integration Leads | Identify automation candidates; lead first-agent experiments; own agents in production |
| Governance / risk team | Establish and evolve agent governance; define safety boundaries; manage escalation protocols |
| L&D / enablement | Deliver agent-design training; facilitate cross-team demonstrations and failure-sharing |
| Executive sponsor | Protect experimentation time; resolve political blockers; celebrate non-engineering team successes |

### Success Signals — Ready to Advance When:

- [ ] Multiple non-engineering teams have built and operate production agents
- [ ] Agent governance exists and evolves based on real experience (not theory)
- [ ] People understand the difference between "using AI tools" and "building AI agents"
- [ ] Role ownership is clear: who builds, who monitors, who's accountable for agent behavior
- [ ] Agents handle genuine repetitive work, freeing teams for higher-judgment tasks
- [ ] Agent failures are detected, shared, and learned from without blame
- [ ] Cross-departmental interest in agents is growing organically

### Watchpoints

| Risk | Signal | Response |
|------|--------|----------|
| Only engineering teams build agents | 6 months in, no non-technical team has a working agent | Diagnose: is it platform access? Capability? Permission? Time? Usually it's that the tools require too much technical skill. |
| "Unsafe" agents built to check a box | Teams rush agents into production without proper failure handling | Strengthen the "safe-to-fail" scaffolding. Slow down scope, not timeline. Better to have 3 reliable simple agents than 10 brittle complex ones. |
| Governance blocks experimentation | Rules written for Level 4 maturity applied to Level 3 experiments | Reset governance to minimum viable. Ask: "what's the minimum oversight needed for this specific agent at this scope?" |
| Learned helplessness from prior failures | Teams tried automation before (RPA, etc.) and it failed; they expect agents to fail too | Acknowledge the history explicitly. Show how this is different. Start with absurdly simple first agents that can't fail. Build from there. |

## Phase 4: Restructure, Govern, and Transform (Quarter 6–8+)

### Level: AI as the System — running critical workflows, scaling organizational capacity

> **TL;DR:** Shift from departmental agent pockets to enterprise-wide AI-native operations. This is primarily a political and organizational design challenge, not a technical one. Restructure incentives, governance, and power dynamics. Support organizational identity transformation. Prove the model with one high-visibility cross-department process before scaling.

### Focus

The transition from Level 3 to Level 4 is the most dangerous. The organization's immune system will fight back. Departmental success creates silos — each team has agents, but they don't coordinate across boundaries. Incentive structures punish the disruption that transformation requires. Political dynamics around power redistribution surface. This is fundamentally an organizational design problem, not a technology problem. The technical infrastructure matters, but it's the easier part.

### Concrete Changes

**Month 1–4: Create the conditions (Environmental Restructuring)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Restructure incentives for transformation leaders | People leading enterprise AI transformation must not be penalized for short-term disruption. Explicitly adjust their evaluation criteria: they are measured on transformation outcomes over 12–18 months, not quarterly stability. |
| Identify one high-visibility cross-department process | Choose a process that crosses at least 2 department boundaries, is visibly inefficient, and has willing stakeholders. This becomes the proof-of-concept for enterprise AI-native operations. |
| Map political dynamics | Before attempting cross-boundary work, understand: who gains power? Who loses it? Who controls budgets and headcount? What informal power structures exist? Address these directly, not by pretending they don't exist. |
| Build enterprise governance architecture | Move beyond team-level agent governance to enterprise-level: data flows across boundaries, shared AI services, cross-department accountability, escalation across reporting lines. This requires executive commitment and active sponsorship. |
| Create cross-department coordination structures | Establish forums (councils, working groups, shared OKRs) that incentivize collaboration across boundaries. Without these, departments will optimize locally and resist enterprise integration. |

**Month 5–8: Build capability (Education + Enablement)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Systems thinking education for leaders | Leaders need to reason about emergent behavior, cascading failures, and unintended consequences of interconnected AI agents operating at enterprise scale. This is a qualitatively different capability from managing departmental agents. |
| Enterprise AI platform build-out | Create shared infrastructure: common agent frameworks, shared data pipelines, cross-department APIs, unified monitoring and observability. Make it technically possible for agents to coordinate across boundaries. |
| Pilot the cross-department process | Run the selected process AI-native for a bounded period with full governance, monitoring, and human oversight. Document everything: what works, what breaks, what governance gaps emerge, what political resistance surfaces. |
| Build new roles | Enterprise AI maturity creates new roles that don't exist at Level 3: AI governance architects, cross-department coordination managers, systems behavior analysts, human-AI interface designers. Staff them. |

**Month 9–12+: Transform and scale (Modelling + Persuasion)**

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Demonstrate the proof-of-concept | When the pilot cross-department process works, make it loudly visible. Show: what used to take N people and M days now takes a fraction, with better quality and full auditability. |
| Scale the model | Apply the governance, coordination, and platform patterns proven in the pilot to 2–3 additional cross-department processes. Iterate the governance framework based on each new process's unique challenges. |
| Support organizational identity transformation | The deepest work: help the organization answer "what does human work mean when AI runs critical operations?" This is not a communications exercise — it's genuine identity work that requires time, space, and honesty. |
| Reference case development | Document the journey for internal and external use. Share with peer organizations. Create a community of practice among enterprise AI-native organizations facing similar transitions. |

### Who Owns It

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| C-suite sponsor (CEO or COO-level) | Active sponsorship; incentive restructuring; political navigation; resource commitment |
| Organizational design team | Cross-department coordination structures; new role design; governance architecture |
| Enterprise AI platform team | Shared infrastructure; cross-boundary technical enablement |
| Governance architects | Enterprise AI governance framework; risk management at scale |
| Systems thinkers / complexity advisors | Help leaders reason about emergent behavior, cascading risks, and unintended consequences |
| Department heads (collectively) | Willingness to cede some autonomy for enterprise-level coordination |

### Success Signals:

- [ ] At least one critical cross-department process runs AI-native with full governance
- [ ] Enterprise governance framework exists and handles cross-boundary accountability
- [ ] Incentive structures reward transformation, not just stability
- [ ] Leaders can reason about system-level AI behavior (not just departmental agents)
- [ ] New roles exist and are staffed (governance architects, coordination managers)
- [ ] The organization has answered — genuinely, not superficially — "what do humans do here?"
- [ ] AI-native processes demonstrate measurably better outcomes than their predecessors

### Watchpoints

| Risk | Signal | Response |
|------|--------|----------|
| Political resistance disguised as "governance concerns" | Endless review cycles, ever-expanding requirements, no actual progress | Name the dynamic. Distinguish legitimate governance needs from political blocking. Escalate to sponsor. |
| Transformation leaders burn out or leave | People driving change are exhausted by resistance; key hires depart | Restructure their support systems and incentives. They need organizational cover, not just mandates. |
| Shadow processes emerge | Departments publicly support enterprise AI but secretly maintain parallel manual processes | Diagnose: is this a trust problem (agents aren't reliable enough) or a power problem (departments don't want to cede control)? Different diagnosis, different response. |
| "AI as the system" becomes a technology project | Massive platform investment without organizational change; engineering delivers infrastructure nobody uses | Refocus: this is an organizational design challenge that requires technology, not a technology challenge that requires organizational buy-in. |
| Regression under stress | A high-profile AI failure causes organization-wide retrenchment to Level 2–3 behaviors | Build resilience into the governance model: clear incident response, blameless postmortems, transparent communication. One failure should strengthen governance, not kill the program. |

## Cross-Phase Dependencies and Sequencing

```
Quarter 1          Quarter 2          Quarter 3          Quarter 4          Quarter 5          Quarter 6          Quarter 7          Quarter 8
├─ Phase 1 ────────┤                                    
         ├── Phase 2 ─────────────────┤                 
                            ├── Phase 3 ─────────────────────────┤
                                                   ├── Phase 4 ──────────────────────────────────────┤
```

**Key sequencing rules:**

1. **Phase 2 begins mid-Phase 1** — once early teams have first wins, start embedding workflows for those teams while other teams are still getting first wins.
2. **Phase 3 begins mid-Phase 2** — once several teams have embedded AI in workflows, the most advanced teams can start agent experimentation while others are still normalizing tool use.
3. **Phase 4 begins mid-Phase 3** — once departmental agents are working, enterprise-level coordination work can begin while other departments are still building their first agents.
4. **Every phase keeps running** — you never "finish" Phase 1. New hires, new teams, and new use cases continuously cycle through earlier phases. The organization operates at multiple levels simultaneously.

## Ongoing Activities (All Phases)

These run continuously across all phases:

| Activity | Purpose | Cadence |
|----------|---------|---------|
| AI showcase / demo day | Visibility, social learning, celebration | Monthly |
| Governance retrospective | Evolve rules based on experience | Quarterly |
| Cross-team pattern sharing | Lateral learning, prevent reinvention | Biweekly |
| Identity and craft conversations | Address evolving professional identity | Ongoing, as needed |
| Regression monitoring | Detect backsliding early | Monthly (check usage data + qualitative signals) |
| Leadership modeling | Maintain social permission and norm consistency | Continuous |

## Expertise Evolution

The team driving this work must evolve as the organization advances:

| Phase | Core Expertise Needed |
|-------|----------------------|
| Phase 1 | Behavior change practitioners, learning designers, internal communications |
| Phase 2 | Workflow designers, operations managers, tool administrators |
| Phase 3 | AI engineers, platform architects, domain experts with technical fluency |
| Phase 4 | Organizational designers, governance architects, systems thinkers, political strategists |

**Do not** try to use Phase 1 expertise (change management, communications) to drive Phase 4 outcomes. Hire or develop the expertise each phase requires.

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid at Every Phase

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails | What to do instead |
|--------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Mandating AI use | Creates compliance theater; deepens anxiety; produces unsafe outcomes at higher levels | Enable, demonstrate, restructure the environment — never coerce |
| Training without environmental support | People learn skills they can't apply because the environment doesn't support the behavior | Fix the environment first, then build capability |
| Enterprise programs before individual experience | Top-down rollouts fail when people haven't personally experienced AI value | Scaffolded first wins → embedded habits → team workflows → enterprise processes |
| Governance before practice | Rules written without experience are either too restrictive or miss real risks | Lightweight governance early; robust frameworks after accumulated experience |
| Treating all levels with the same intervention | Lunch-and-learns don't solve agent-building capability gaps; workflow tools don't solve political dynamics | Match the intervention to the level's specific behavioral barriers |
| Ignoring identity dynamics | "Just use the tool" doesn't work when professional self-concept is at stake | Actively support identity evolution at every level |

## Measuring Progress

### Leading Indicators (early signals of progress)

| Level | Leading Indicators |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1 → 2 | Sharing behavior emerging; people asking each other "how did you do that?"; AI visible in team meetings |
| 2 → 3 | Teams identifying automation candidates without being asked; AI Integration Leads proposing agents; curiosity about "what else could we automate?" |
| 3 → 4 | Teams noticing cross-boundary friction; department leads asking about coordination; platform requests from multiple teams simultaneously |

### Lagging Indicators (confirm progress after the fact)

| Level | Lagging Indicators |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1 achieved | >50% weekly active AI users; organic sharing channels active; no confusion about permission |
| 2 achieved | Measurable time savings at team level; AI-default workflows documented and onboarded; calibration capability demonstrated |
| 3 achieved | Production agents running in non-engineering teams; governance evolving from experience; agent failures handled without panic |
| 4 achieved | Cross-department AI processes running; enterprise governance operational; new roles staffed and functioning; organizational identity work underway |
