# Combined COM-B Report: AI Maturity Model — Cross-Level Synthesis

**Maturity model:** [The AI Transformation Model](https://notion-ai-assessment.vercel.app/) by John Hurley, Notion  
**Diagnostic tool:** [Change Lenses and Actions](https://github.com/johnpcutler/change-lenses-and-actions) — seven-step behavior diagnosis framework (30+ sub-lenses, 150+ dimensional scales, 9 intervention functions, 93 behaviour change techniques)  
**Behavioral framework:** COM-B (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation → Behavior) → Behaviour Change Wheel → Behaviour Change Technique Taxonomy v1  
**Scope:** Four-level AI maturity model analyzed through behavioral diagnostics  
**Date:** 2026-05-09

---

## Executive Summary

> **TL;DR:** Opportunity — not motivation or capability — is the primary barrier at every AI maturity level. The blockers are structural, social, and political, not attitudinal. Organizations don't fail at AI adoption because people don't want it; they fail because the environment makes it difficult, invisible, unrewarded, or unsafe.

This report synthesizes COM-B behavioral diagnoses across all four levels of the [AI Transformation Model](https://notion-ai-assessment.vercel.app/) (John Hurley, Notion):

1. **AI as a Thought Partner** — help people explore ideas and improve decisions
2. **AI as an Assistant** — complete individual tasks faster, save employee time
3. **AI as Teammates** — automate repetitive work, increase team efficiency
4. **AI as the System** — run critical workflows, scale organizational capacity

The most striking finding across all four levels: **Opportunity is the primary barrier at every stage of maturity.** The dominant blockers are never "people don't want to use AI" or even "people can't use AI" — they are structural, social, and political conditions that make the target behavior difficult, invisible, unrewarded, or unsafe. This has profound implications for how organizations design AI adoption programs.

## Cross-Level COM-B Rankings

> **TL;DR:** Opportunity dominates at every level (Opportunity > Capability > Motivation). The lead intervention function shifts from Modelling (Level 1) to Enablement (Levels 2-3) to Environmental Restructuring (Level 4) as the challenge deepens from social permission to structural transformation.

| Level | #1 Branch | #2 Branch | #3 Branch | Intervention Function Priority |
|-------|-----------|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 — Thought Partner | Opportunity | Capability | Motivation | Modelling > Enablement > Environmental Restructuring > Education > Persuasion |
| 2 — Assistant | Opportunity | Capability | Motivation | Enablement > Environmental Restructuring > Modelling > Training > Persuasion |
| 3 — Teammates | Opportunity | Capability | Motivation | Enablement > Environmental Restructuring > Modelling > Training > Persuasion |
| 4 — The System | Opportunity | Capability | Motivation | Environmental Restructuring > Enablement > Education > Modelling > Persuasion |

The ranking is consistent: **Opportunity > Capability > Motivation** at every level. But the *nature* of the opportunity barrier, the *type* of capability gap, and the *character* of the motivation challenge shift dramatically across levels.

## How Barriers Evolve Across Levels

> **TL;DR:** Opportunity barriers escalate from passive ambiguity to active political resistance. Capability shifts from individual skill to organizational systems thinking. Motivation deepens from personal identity questioning to existential organizational threat.

### Opportunity Barriers: From Permission to Politics

| Level | Primary Opportunity Barrier | Character |
|-------|---------------------------|-----------|
| 1 | Social permission — unclear norms, absent rules, invisible success | **Permission vacuum** |
| 2 | Structural defaults — invisible gains, misaligned incentives, governance gaps | **Neutral-to-hostile environment** |
| 3 | Infrastructure + role ambiguity — missing platforms, contested ownership, premature governance | **Structural exclusion** |
| 4 | Political dynamics — power redistribution, accountability gaps, entrenched incentives | **Active immune response** |

The opportunity barrier escalates from *passive absence* (Level 1: nobody said you can't) to *active resistance* (Level 4: the organizational immune system fights back). At Level 1, opportunity is mostly about removing ambiguity. By Level 4, opportunity requires restructuring power relationships.

### Capability Barriers: From Fluency to Systems Thinking

| Level | Primary Capability Gap | Character |
|-------|----------------------|-----------|
| 1 | Procedural fluency — people can't prompt effectively or judge AI output | **Individual skill gap** |
| 2 | Calibration + mental models — over-trust, under-trust, and no shared standards | **Judgment gap** |
| 3 | Multi-layered technical + judgment — agent design, failure detection, workflow selection | **Team technical capability** |
| 4 | Systems reasoning — emergent behavior, cascading failures, governance architecture | **Organizational metacognition** |

Capability gaps shift from *individual skill* to *collective intelligence*. At Level 1, one person needs to learn to prompt. At Level 4, the entire organization needs to reason about complex adaptive systems. The nature of the required expertise is qualitatively different at each level — Level 3 and 4 capabilities cannot be taught through the same mechanisms that work for Levels 1 and 2.

### Motivation Barriers: From Identity to Existential Threat

| Level | Primary Motivation Barrier | Character |
|-------|--------------------------|-----------|
| 1 | Low self-efficacy + identity threat for senior professionals | **"AI isn't for me"** |
| 2 | Identity conflict + competing habits + rational cost-benefit | **"Not worth the effort"** |
| 3 | Low efficacy for non-engineers + learned helplessness from prior failures + identity exclusion | **"This isn't my job"** |
| 4 | Organizational identity crisis + existential threat + controlled motivation | **"This threatens who we are"** |

Motivation barriers escalate from *personal identity questioning* to *organizational existential crisis*. At Level 1, individuals wonder "does AI fit who I am?" At Level 4, the entire organization confronts "what does human work mean in an AI-native world?" Each level adds emotional weight and existential depth to the motivation challenge.

## The Progression Paradox

> **TL;DR:** What works at one level becomes the obstacle at the next. Ad-hoc exploration blocks formalization. Individual mastery blocks workflow redesign. Departmental success blocks enterprise coordination. You can't "do more of what worked" — each transition demands a qualitative shift.

A critical finding: **what makes an organization succeed at Level N often blocks progression to Level N+1.**

| Transition | What Helped at Previous Level | Why It Blocks the Next Level |
|-----------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 1 → 2 | Individual exploration and ad-hoc experimentation | Lack of structure, shared practices, and governance prevents formalization |
| 2 → 3 | Individual tool mastery and personal productivity gains | Individual-level optimization doesn't translate to team-level workflow redesign; scaffold-dependence prevents independence |
| 3 → 4 | Team-level agent autonomy and departmental automation | Departmental success creates silos; local governance doesn't scale; political boundaries emerge at enterprise transitions |

This paradox means organizations cannot simply "do more of what worked" to advance. Each level transition requires a qualitative shift in approach, not just quantitative scaling of the previous level's interventions.

## Recurring Patterns Across All Four Levels

> **TL;DR:** Five patterns recur at every maturity level: (1) AI success is invisible — make it visible, (2) leader rhetoric contradicts operational reality — actions must match words, (3) identity threat deepens with each level — address it directly, (4) coercion backfires universally — never mandate, and (5) entrenched habits resist displacement regardless of attitude change — restructure the environment.

### 1. The Visibility Problem

At every level, AI-assisted success is invisible:
- **Level 1:** Individual AI use is private; colleagues can't see it working
- **Level 2:** Productivity gains are invisible to managers and peers
- **Level 3:** Agent automation operates in the background; the work it replaces was also invisible
- **Level 4:** Enterprise-wide AI coordination is too complex for any single person to observe

**Implication:** Making AI value visible — through demonstrations, dashboards, showcases, and storytelling — is a universal lever. Modelling appears in the top 4 intervention functions at every level.

### 2. The Norm Consistency Gap

At every level, leadership messaging contradicts operational reality:
- **Level 1:** "Embrace AI" + "Did you write this yourself?"
- **Level 2:** "Use AI tools" + performance reviews ignore AI fluency
- **Level 3:** "Build agents" + incentives reward manual delivery, not automation investment
- **Level 4:** "Be AI-native" + managers evaluated on near-term stability

**Implication:** Until leader *behavior* matches leader *rhetoric*, norm-based adoption stalls. Demonstration of the behavior from leaders — not just endorsement — is required at every level.

### 3. The Identity Escalation

Identity threat deepens with each level:
- **Level 1:** "Am I admitting I'm not smart enough?"
- **Level 2:** "Am I undermining my craft?"
- **Level 3:** "Am I automating myself out of a job?"
- **Level 4:** "Does human expertise matter anymore?"

**Implication:** Identity work (through framing/reframing and identity-association techniques) must be calibrated to the existential depth of each level. The same reframe ("AI augments, doesn't replace") works at Level 1 but rings hollow by Level 4. Higher levels require genuinely answering the identity question, not just reframing it.

### 4. Coercion is Contraindicated at Every Level

All four analyses independently conclude that mandating AI adoption is counterproductive:
- At Level 1: creates compliance theater and deepens anxiety
- At Level 2: triggers identity backlash and controlled motivation
- At Level 3: produces unsafe agents built to check a box
- At Level 4: generates shadow processes and political resistance

**Implication:** AI maturity cannot be mandated. It must be enabled, demonstrated, restructured into the environment, and supported — but never coerced. This is the single most consistent finding across all four analyses.

### 5. The Habit Layer Persists

At every level, entrenched existing behaviors resist displacement:
- **Level 1:** Google search, blank-page drafting, solo brainstorming
- **Level 2:** Manual writing, manual note-taking, manual search
- **Level 3:** Manual workflows, manual routing, manual triage
- **Level 4:** Manual oversight, manual approvals, departmental handoffs

**Implication:** Environmental Restructuring to disrupt existing cues, combined with deliberate habit formation anchored to existing triggers, is required at every level. Habits don't yield to information or persuasion — they yield to environmental change and deliberate replacement.

## Escalating Intervention Architecture

> **TL;DR:** Level 1 needs permission and first wins (leader modeling). Level 2 needs embedding and normalizing (workflow defaults + ownership). Level 3 needs scaffolding and governance (safe-to-fail zones + first agents). Level 4 needs restructuring and identity transformation (incentive realignment + political redesign).

Based on the cross-level analysis, the intervention strategy must evolve as organizations advance:

### Level 1 Intervention Profile: "Permission and First Wins"

| Priority | Function | Core Mechanism |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| 1 | Modelling | Leader modeling to close the norm-consistency gap |
| 2 | Enablement | Scaffolded first-win experiences to break the fluency-efficacy spiral |
| 3 | Environmental Restructuring | Clear, permissive guidelines to create safe-to-experiment zones |
| 4 | Education | Practical mental model primers (not AI literacy courses) |
| 5 | Persuasion | Identity reframing for senior professionals |

**Signature move:** A senior leader publicly shares their own AI use (including imperfect results) in a team meeting.

### Level 2 Intervention Profile: "Embed and Normalize"

| Priority | Function | Core Mechanism |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| 1 | Enablement | Scaffold AI into workflow defaults; action planning for if-then habits |
| 2 | Environmental Restructuring | Restructure incentives, assign ownership, create visibility dashboards |
| 3 | Modelling | Identity-congruent peer demonstrations across roles |
| 4 | Training | Graded tasks using real work, embedded in workflow (not classroom) |
| 5 | Persuasion | Reframe identity ("AI makes great writers better") + address job anxiety |

**Signature move:** Every team has an "AI Integration Lead" who owns making AI the default for 2-3 team workflows.

### Level 3 Intervention Profile: "Scaffold, Protect, and Govern"

| Priority | Function | Core Mechanism |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| 1 | Enablement | Scaffolded first-agent experiences for non-engineering teams |
| 2 | Environmental Restructuring | Assign ownership, resolve role ambiguity, create safe-to-fail zones |
| 3 | Modelling | Cross-team demonstrations of successful agents from non-technical teams |
| 4 | Training | Hands-on agent-building with graduated complexity |
| 5 | Persuasion | Reframe agent-building as domain expertise amplification |

**Signature move:** A non-engineering team builds and deploys their first production agent with scaffolded support in under 2 weeks.

### Level 4 Intervention Profile: "Restructure, Govern, and Transform"

| Priority | Function | Core Mechanism |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| 1 | Environmental Restructuring | Restructure incentives, governance, and power dynamics for enterprise AI |
| 2 | Enablement | Enterprise platforms, cross-department coordination, governance frameworks |
| 3 | Education | Systems thinking education for leaders (emergent behavior, cascading failure) |
| 4 | Modelling | Reference cases from AI-native organizations; future-state demonstrations |
| 5 | Persuasion | Organizational identity work: "what humans do in an AI-native org" |

**Signature move:** Restructure one high-visibility cross-department process to run AI-native with full governance, proving the model works.

## The Critical Transitions

> **TL;DR:** Each transition has a defining shift and a predictable trap. 1→2: individual to shared (trap: formalizing before experience). 2→3: tool use to automation (trap: assuming prompting skills transfer to agent design). 3→4: departmental to enterprise (trap: treating it as a technology problem when it's political).

### Transition 1 → 2: "From Individual to Shared"

**What must change:** AI use must shift from private, ad-hoc exploration to embedded, visible, team-level practice.

**Key interventions at the boundary:**
- Establish shared vocabulary and behavior specifications (Psychological Capability: shared definitions)
- Assign ownership for AI integration at team level (Social Opportunity: role clarity)
- Create governance that enables rather than restricts (Social Opportunity: norms and rules)
- Make individual gains visible to create social learning (Physical Opportunity: visibility → Social Opportunity: observability)

**The trap:** Trying to formalize before enough people have genuine experience. If standards are set by people who haven't used AI deeply, the standards will be wrong.

### Transition 2 → 3: "From Tool Use to Automation"

**What must change:** AI must shift from assisting individual tasks to autonomously executing multi-step workflows.

**Key interventions at the boundary:**
- Build technical capability in non-engineering teams (Psychological Capability: procedural knowledge + skills)
- Resolve the ownership question — who builds agents? (Social Opportunity: role clarity)
- Create "safe-to-fail" experimentation zones for agent development (Social Opportunity: psychological safety)
- Develop governance that evolves with practice, not imposed in advance (Social Opportunity: norms and rules)
- Address learned helplessness from prior automation failures (Automatic Motivation: learned helplessness)

**The trap:** Expecting Level 2 skills (prompting, evaluating output) to transfer directly to Level 3 requirements (agent design, monitoring, failure handling). They don't — it's a qualitative capability jump.

### Transition 3 → 4: "From Departmental to Enterprise"

**What must change:** AI automation must cross organizational boundaries, require new governance structures, and redefine human roles.

**Key interventions at the boundary:**
- Restructure incentives so transformation leaders aren't punished for disruption (Social Opportunity: incentive alignment)
- Build enterprise-wide governance for AI autonomy (Social Opportunity: norms and rules)
- Address the political dynamics of power redistribution (Social Opportunity: psychological safety)
- Support organizational identity work — "what are we in an AI-native world?" (Reflective Motivation: identity)
- Correct the "AI as tool" mental model toward "AI as operating system" (Psychological Capability: mental models)

**The trap:** Treating Level 4 as a technology problem when it is fundamentally an organizational identity and political problem. The technical infrastructure matters, but it's the easier part.

## Unified Intervention Principles

> **TL;DR:** Six universal principles: (1) fix environment before people, (2) show don't tell, (3) identity work is load-bearing, (4) scaffolded first wins before programs, (5) governance co-evolves with practice, and (6) each level requires fundamentally different expertise.

Based on the cross-level synthesis, these principles apply regardless of maturity level:

### 1. Fix the Environment Before the People

At every level, opportunity barriers outweigh capability and motivation barriers. Investing in training, persuasion, or awareness while the environment is neutral-to-hostile is waste. The sequence is always: **create the conditions** → **build the capability** → **sustain the motivation**.

### 2. Start with Visibility, Not Education

Information doesn't drive behavior. Observation does. At every level, making the target behavior visible (through demonstrations, dashboards, and showcases) is higher-leverage than explaining why people should do it.

### 3. Identity is Never Optional

From Level 1 through Level 4, identity dynamics are active. "Just use the tool" ignores that people's professional self-concept determines whether they'll engage. Identity work is not a soft, optional addition to the intervention — it is load-bearing infrastructure.

### 4. Scaffolded First Wins Before Scaled Programs

At every level, the most effective pattern is: help people succeed once with real work → let success build efficacy → expand from there. Comprehensive programs, certifications, and enterprise-wide rollouts fail when they precede personal experience of value.

### 5. Governance Must Co-Evolve with Practice

Governance imposed before experience produces restrictive rules that block adoption. Governance absent during scaling produces chaos and risk. The resolution: lightweight, permissive governance early (Levels 1-2) that evolves into robust frameworks as organizational experience accumulates (Levels 3-4).

### 6. Each Level Requires Different Expertise

- **Level 1:** Behavior change practitioners, learning designers, communications professionals
- **Level 2:** Workflow designers, operations managers, tool administrators
- **Level 3:** AI engineers, platform architects, domain experts with technical fluency
- **Level 4:** Organizational designers, governance architects, systems thinkers, political strategists

Organizations that try to use Level 1 expertise (change management, communications) to drive Level 4 transformation will fail. The expertise must escalate with the challenge.

## The Intervention Function Shift

> **TL;DR:** The lead intervention function shifts from Modelling → Enablement → Environmental Restructuring as you climb levels. Persuasion ranks dead last at every level — you cannot persuade your way to AI maturity.

Mapping intervention function priority across levels reveals a clear pattern:

```
Level 1:  Modelling > Enablement > Environmental Restructuring > Education > Persuasion
          ("see others do it")

Level 2:  Enablement > Environmental Restructuring > Modelling > Training > Persuasion
          ("make it easier")

Level 3:  Enablement > Environmental Restructuring > Modelling > Training > Persuasion
          ("make it possible")

Level 4:  Environmental Restructuring > Enablement > Education > Modelling > Persuasion
          ("change the system")
```

The shift from Modelling → Enablement → Environmental Restructuring across levels reflects the deepening structural challenge:
- At Level 1, the behavior is simple enough that seeing others do it is sufficient to unblock
- At Levels 2-3, the behavior requires structural support (tools, scaffolds, time, ownership)
- At Level 4, the behavior requires restructuring the organizational system itself

**Persuasion ranks last at every level.** This is the most important cross-level finding for practitioners: you cannot persuade your way to AI maturity. Arguments, reframing, and motivational messaging have their place, but they are always less effective than structural, environmental, and social interventions.

## Risk Patterns

> **TL;DR:** Three risk categories. Moving too fast: skipping levels produces unreliable agents or top-down infrastructure with no adoption. Getting stuck: plateaus produce "everyone uses AI but nothing changed" stagnation. Regression: under stress, organizations default to the previous level's behavior.

### Risk of Moving Too Fast

Organizations that skip levels face predictable failures:
- **Skipping 1 → jumping to 3:** Teams build agents without the foundational prompting skills or mental models to design them well. Result: unreliable agents that erode trust.
- **Skipping 2 → jumping to 4:** Enterprise AI transformation without embedded individual AI habits. Result: top-down infrastructure with no bottom-up adoption.

### Risk of Getting Stuck

Organizations that plateau at each level face predictable stagnation patterns:
- **Stuck at Level 1:** Scattered individual exploration that never compounds. Organization talks about AI but nothing changes operationally.
- **Stuck at Level 2:** Individual productivity gains that remain siloed. No organizational learning. "Everyone uses Copilot but nothing about how we work has changed."
- **Stuck at Level 3:** Departmental automation pockets that can't coordinate. Each team has agents; the organization doesn't.

### Risk of Regression

Under stress, organizations regress to the previous level:
- **3 → 2 regression:** When an agent fails visibly, teams revert to manual processes and individual tool use.
- **2 → 1 regression:** When incentives shift (new leadership, budget cuts), embedded AI tools get abandoned and usage returns to ad-hoc.
- **1 → 0 regression:** When anxiety peaks (layoff announcements, AI incidents in the news), people stop using AI entirely.

## Conclusion

> **TL;DR:** AI maturity is a behavioral challenge, not a technology challenge. The answer at every level: create opportunity first, build capability through practice, sustain motivation through identity-compatible framing. The intervention that works at one level will fail at the next — match the intervention to the level.

AI maturity is fundamentally a behavioral challenge at every level. The technology is the easy part — it's available, improving rapidly, and increasingly accessible. What's hard is changing how people work, how organizations reward behavior, how power and authority are distributed, and how professional identity adapts.

The COM-B framework reveals that the answer at every level is the same structural pattern: **create the opportunity** (restructure the environment so the behavior is easier, safer, and more rewarded), **build the capability** (through scaffolded practice, not classroom education), and **sustain the motivation** (through identity-compatible framing, visible success, and social support — never through coercion).

The critical insight for practitioners: **the intervention that works at one level will fail at the next.** Leader modeling drives Level 1. Enablement and restructuring drive Levels 2-3. Political and governance restructuring drives Level 4. An organization that applies Level 1 tactics (awareness campaigns, lunch-and-learns) to Level 3 problems (agent-building capability, role ambiguity) will waste resources and deepen frustration. Match the intervention to the level — and to the specific COM-B barriers the diagnostic reveals.
